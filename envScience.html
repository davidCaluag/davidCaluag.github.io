Microplastics

What is the Issue?

"Since [the 1930s,] the polymers industry has completely altered [our] daily life: Plastics are in our homes, cars, clothes, furniture, electronics [--they are, as well, in] our single-use throwaway water bottles, food packaging and takeout containers." "Microplastics and nanoplastics [have derived] from primary and secondary sources like [large] plastic chunks and "[we are detecting them] in [many different types of environment] across the globe, from ocean bottoms to mountain peaks." Microplastics, a combination of nano plastics, are found in length ranging from 1–1000 micrometres (μm)-- originally(2023) 5000μm, while nano plastics peak to 100 nanometres (nm). Others have argued that nano plastic's peak length is 1000 nanometres or 1 micrometre, but it will quietly digress.

In 2023, researchers have seen an "emergence of microplastics in the human body [...Found in places like] feces, blood, placenta, lower airway, and lungs." The future's trajectory is alarming because of how rapidly it may negatively impact the environment and public health. "Notably, the entry of plastics into the gastrointestinal tract can result in various ailments, including dysbiosis of gut microflora and microbial biodiversity." Constant/prolonged exposure may "potentially [lead] to various negative effects, such as reduced growth rates and energy levels, physiological stress, cell death, developmental abnormalities, altered lipid metabolism, and intestinal damage." Additionally, "an increased risk of stomach cancer noticed with more prolonged exposure to synthetic [fibre] dust." Microplastics can directly cause "oxidative stress and the antioxidant defence system failure." Oxidative stress "plays a role in many conditions like cancer, Alzheimer's disease and heart disease. Toxins like pollution and cigarette smoke can cause oxidative stress, while foods rich in antioxidants can help reduce it."

"Due to the widespread distribution and persistent existence of [microplastics] in the environment, there is an urgent necessity to comprehensively understand [and reverse] the potentially harmful effects and mechanisms of MPs (including smaller NPs) on human health." 

Note: 1 mm = 1000 μm ; 1 μm = 1000 nm;

How Does It Affect the Environment?

1) Pollution in Water Bodies

  • Microplastics contaminate oceans, rivers, and lakes, harming marine life.
  • Marine animals ingest microplastics, leading to blockages, starvation, and toxic buildup.
  • Plastic waste breaks down into microplastics, entering the food chain.

    • 2) Soil and Agriculture Damage

  • Microplastics are found in farmland soil due to fertilizers, sewage sludge, and plastic waste.
  • They reduce soil fertility by affecting microbial activity and nutrient absorption.
  • Crops absorb microplastics, interfering with plant growth and food production.

    • 3) Air Pollution & Climate Impact

  • Microplastics are found in the air and travel long distances through wind.
  • Burning plastic releases toxic chemicals contributing to global warming and respiratory diseases.
  • They disrupt cloud formation and weather patterns, impacting rainfall and ecosystems.

    • 4) Impact on Photosynthesis (New Study)

  • Microplastics hinder plant photosynthesis, reducing oxygen production.
  • Crop yields may decrease by 4%-14%, putting millions at risk of food shortages.

    • Read more: PIRG Article, Guardian Study

    What Is Its Impact Socially and Economically?

    Social Impacts

  • Health Concerns: Microplastics are found in food, water, and human tissues, raising concerns about long-term health effects.
  • Public Awareness: Growing awareness has led to increased demand for sustainable products and stricter plastic regulations.
  • Environmental Justice: Communities near polluted areas face disproportionate exposure, exacerbating social inequalities.

    • Economic Impacts

  • Cost of Cleanup: Governments spend significant resources on cleaning up microplastic pollution.
  • Impact on Industries: Fisheries and tourism suffer due to polluted waters and declining marine biodiversity.
  • Regulatory Costs: Industries face increased costs to comply with microplastic pollution regulations.

    • Sources: MDPI Study, ScienceDirect Research

    What Solutions Are Being Tried or Proposed?

    When plastic-based products break down, they do not biodegrade instead, they break down into microplastics, which are the size of a sesame seed or smaller. They are made of numerous different toxic chemical components, making them capable of poisoning everything they touch. Worst of all? They are literally everywhere. Here are some solutions that could be done:

    1.Banning certain microplastics might work (but only if companies get on board)

    In 2020, the European Union (EU) released a sweeping ban on microplastics which covered about 90 percent of all plastic pollutants. According to The Guardian, the main goal of this ban was to cut plastic pollution by about 400,000 tons over the intervening two decades. On average, about 36,000 tons of microplastic fibers and fragments a year would no longer be permitted to be “intentionally added” to products.

    2. Plastic microfiber filtration could help scoop up the problem at home

    The solution to this particular problem could be to develop house-based filtration systems capable of capturing these microplastics before they make their way into the sewer. If that works, such technology could be used to solve other "particulate" problems and be repurposed as a means to remove other types of microplastics from waterways worldwide. As it happens, this technology does exist in some form or another.

    3. Magnets could be employed to attract microplastics

    In 2019, the Google Science Fair winner was 18-year-old Fionn Ferreira, of West Cork, Ireland. His experiment proved that using magnetite powder, a type of iron oxide, could be used to clean up oil spills.

    4. Mass Cleanups (Best Solution So Far)

    The Great Pacific Garbage Patch is a big problem all its own, and we can only solve that problem by cleaning things up before it gets any worse. Once that plastic becomes microplastic, it becomes nearly impossible to clean up. Thus, the solution could be removing plastic from the ocean before it becomes micro in the first place. Charities and environmental groups are already working to fix the problem as best they can.
