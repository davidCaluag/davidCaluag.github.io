Microplastics

What is the Issue?

"Since [the 1930s,] the polymers industry has completely altered [our] daily life: Plastics are in our homes, cars, clothes, furniture, electronics [--they are, as well, in] our single-use throwaway water bottles, food packaging and takeout containers.” “Microplastics and nanoplastics [have derived] from primary and secondary sources like [large] plastic chunks and "[we’re detecting them] in [many different types of environment] across the globe, from ocean bottoms to mountain peaks." Microplastics, a combination of nano plastics, are found in length ranging from 1–1000 micrometres (μm), while nano plastics peak to 100 nanometres (nm). Others have argued that nano plastic's peak length is 1000 nanometres or 1 micrometre, but it'll quietly digress. In 2023, researchers have seen an "emergence of microplastics in the human body [...Found in places like] feces, blood, placenta, lower airway, and lungs". The future's trajectory is alarming because of how rapidly it may negatively impact the environment and public health. "Notably, the entry of plastics into the gastrointestinal tract can result in various ailments, including dysbiosis of gut microflora and microbial biodiversity." Constant/prolonged exposure has "potentially [lead] to various negative effects, such as reduced growth rates and energy levels, physiological stress, cell death, developmental abnormalities, altered lipid metabolism, and intestinal damage." And "due [to its] widespread distribution and persistent existence [...in the environment], there is an urgent necessity to comprehensively understand [and reverse] the potentially harmful effects and mechanisms of MPs on [the health of living things]."

How Does It Affect the Environment?

1) Pollution in Water Bodies

  • Microplastics contaminate oceans, rivers, and lakes, harming marine life.
  • Marine animals ingest microplastics, leading to blockages, starvation, and toxic buildup.
  • Plastic waste breaks down into microplastics, entering the food chain.

2) Soil and Agriculture Damage

  • Microplastics are found in farmland soil due to fertilizers, sewage sludge, and plastic waste.
  • They reduce soil fertility by affecting microbial activity and nutrient absorption.
  • Crops absorb microplastics, interfering with plant growth and food production.

3) Air Pollution & Climate Impact

  • Microplastics are found in the air and travel long distances through wind.
  • Burning plastic releases toxic chemicals contributing to global warming and respiratory diseases.
  • They disrupt cloud formation and weather patterns, impacting rainfall and ecosystems.

4) Impact on Photosynthesis (New Study)

  • Microplastics hinder plant photosynthesis, reducing oxygen production.
  • Crop yields may decrease by 4%-14%, putting millions at risk of food shortages.

Read more: PIRG Article, Guardian Study

What Is Its Impact Socially and Economically?

Social Impacts

  • Health Concerns: Microplastics are found in food, water, and human tissues, raising concerns about long-term health effects.
  • Public Awareness: Growing awareness has led to increased demand for sustainable products and stricter plastic regulations.
  • Environmental Justice: Communities near polluted areas face disproportionate exposure, exacerbating social inequalities.

Economic Impacts

  • Cost of Cleanup: Governments spend significant resources on cleaning up microplastic pollution.
  • Impact on Industries: Fisheries and tourism suffer due to polluted waters and declining marine biodiversity.
  • Regulatory Costs: Industries face increased costs to comply with microplastic pollution regulations.

Sources: MDPI Study, ScienceDirect Research

What Solutions Are Being Tried or Proposed?

When plastic-based products break down, they do not biodegrade; instead, they fragment into microplastics—tiny particles no larger than a sesame seed. These particles contain toxic chemicals that can contaminate ecosystems. Below are some proposed solutions:

1. Banning Certain Microplastics

In 2020, the European Union (EU) introduced a ban covering 90% of plastic pollutants. The aim was to cut plastic pollution by 400,000 tons over two decades, prohibiting an estimated 36,000 tons of microplastics from being intentionally added to products each year.

2. Plastic Microfiber Filtration

Home-based filtration systems could help capture microplastics before they enter sewer systems. If implemented successfully, this technology could be adapted to remove microplastics from waterways worldwide.

3. Magnetic Attraction of Microplastics

In 2019, Fionn Ferreira, an 18-year-old from West Cork, Ireland, won the Google Science Fair for demonstrating how magnetite powder (a type of iron oxide) can help remove microplastics from water sources.

4. Mass Cleanups

The Great Pacific Garbage Patch highlights the need for large-scale cleanup efforts. Removing plastic waste before it degrades into microplastics is crucial. Environmental groups are actively working on large-scale ocean cleanup projects.
